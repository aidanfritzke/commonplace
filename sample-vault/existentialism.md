# On Existentialism

Existentialism begins where Stoicism leaves off — not with what we cannot control,
but with the vertigo of what we can. Sartre's formula is *existence precedes
essence*: there is no blueprint, no fixed human nature handed down in advance. We
arrive first and invent ourselves afterward, through our choices, and we are
"condemned to be free" — answerable for who we become with no excuse to hide behind.

That freedom is mostly fled. Sartre called the flight *bad faith* — the waiter who
plays at being a waiter, the person who pretends their role, their past, or their
circumstances decided them. Anything to set down the weight of having chosen. To
live authentically is simply to stop pretending the choice was never yours.

Dostoevsky got there first, from the other side. His Underground Man would rather be
spiteful and free than contented and determined; Ivan Karamazov's "if God does not
exist, everything is permitted" is less a permission than a terror — the same
groundlessness Sartre would later embrace. Both saw the same thing: a life without
given meaning is not an excuse to despair but a demand to make one.
